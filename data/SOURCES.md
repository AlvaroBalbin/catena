# Sources, rights, and provenance

This file is the integrity backbone of Catena. Every text in the corpus is listed here with its exact edition, translator, license, and the reasoning for its rights status. **No text enters `data/` until it has an entry here with a verified license.**

Rule: if a text's redistribution rights are unclear, it does not ship. We reference and cite it, we do not redistribute it.

---

## Included: public domain

### Summa Theologica (St. Thomas Aquinas)
- **Translation:** Fathers of the English Dominican Province, second and revised edition, 1920-1922 (Benziger Bros. printing widely dated 1947).
- **License:** Public domain. The translation was published 1920-22; the translators worked as a body ("Fathers of the English Dominican Province") and the work is long out of copyright in the US and EU (published >95 years ago; all contributors' work of this era is public domain).
- **Confirmed by:** Online Library of Liberty and CCEL both host it as public domain. <https://oll.libertyfund.org/titles/province-the-summa-theologica-of-st-thomas-aquinas-part-i-10-vols>
- **Source edition used for ingest:** _to be recorded at ingest time_ (candidate sources: CCEL structured edition, New Advent). We record the exact source URL and retrieval date per unit in the manifest.
- **Attribution redistributed with the text:** "St. Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologica, trans. Fathers of the English Dominican Province, 2nd rev. ed. (1920-22). Public domain."

### Catena Aurea (St. Thomas Aquinas, on the Four Gospels)
- **Translation/Edition:** *Catena Aurea: Commentary on the Four Gospels, collected out of the works of the Fathers*, the Oxford translation edited by John Henry Newman, published by J. G. F. and J. Rivington, London, 1841-1845 (4 volumes, one per Gospel). This is the patristic "golden chain": for each Gospel passage, Aquinas chains verbatim fragments from the Church Fathers (Chrysostom, Augustine, Bede, Jerome, Origen, Ambrose, and many more), each fragment attributed to its Father.
- **License:** Public domain. The translation was published 1841-45 and its editor (Newman, d. 1890) and translators are long deceased; the work is far out of copyright in the US and EU (published >175 years ago). A faithful mechanical transcription of a public-domain text adds no new copyright.
- **Confirmed by:** The Christian Classics Ethereal Library (CCEL) hosts this same Oxford/Rivington edition as public domain (print source recorded in its metadata as "London: J.G.F. and J. Rivington, 1842"). <https://www.ccel.org/ccel/aquinas/catena1.html>
- **Source edition used for ingest:** ecatholic2000.com, which reproduces the Newman Oxford translation with a uniform per-chapter HTML structure keyed to Douay-Rheims verse anchors. Retrieved 2026-07-10; the four Gospels span its `catena/untitled-08.shtml` through `untitled-109.shtml`. Every fetched chapter page is cached under `ingest/.cache/catena_html/`, and the exact chapter URL is recorded per node in the `source.url` field.
- **Confirmed by (verbatim cross-check):** before ingest, ecatholic2000's text was compared fragment-by-fragment against the pristine CCEL ThML edition of the *same* Newman translation (cached at `ingest/.cache/catena/catena1.xml` for Matthew and `catena2.xml` for Mark). Three pericopes were checked (the Jerome / Rabanus / Pseudo-Chrysostom / Chrysostom fragments on Matthew 1:1); the Fathers' commentary is word-for-word identical. The only differences observed are cosmetic and non-substantive: ecatholic2000 flattens the print edition's superscript footnote-reference letters inline (so "in Hebrew[a]" reads "in Hebrewa"), occasionally drops a pair of quotation marks around a quoted phrase, omits CCEL's bracketed editorial `[ed. note: ...]` apparatus (which is not the Father's text), and carries the rare OCR slip (one "he" for "be"). None of this is abridgement or rewording; the commentary is complete and verbatim, so ecatholic2000 was accepted as the ingest source.
- **Scope of what is ingested (an honest boundary, not a silent one):** the citable unit is the **pericope** (one Gospel verse-range with its chain of Father fragments), one node per pericope. Captured verbatim: each Father fragment's text (with only the leading bold Father name stripped off into `segments[].father`, and internal whitespace runs collapsed to single spaces), the Gospel verse text quoted at the head of the block (`lemma`), and the Fathers' own Scripture cross-references found inside the fragments (`citations_out`). The bold Father name that opens each fragment is normalized to a clean display form (e.g. `PSEUDO-CHRYSOSTOM` to `Pseudo-Chrysostom`, `CYRIL OF ALEXANDRIA` to `Cyril of Alexandria`). A fragment with no leading Father name (an Aquinas connective or a continuation paragraph) is appended to the preceding Father rather than invented as its own. Not captured as nodes: the source site's navigation and page furniture, and CCEL's separate editorial footnote apparatus. No fragment text is summarized, truncated, or dropped; the segments of every node rejoin to its `text` exactly, and this is asserted at ingest and re-checked by `ingest/validate.py`. Each pericope is keyed to the Gospel verses it comments (`commented_verse_keys`, the golden-chain join key); because the Fathers read the Vulgate, these keys use the Douay-Rheims/Vulgate versification already in `data/bible/drb/`, and a handful of chapter-end label verses that exist only in modern numbering (Matthew 17:27, Mark 4:41, Mark 9:50, John 11:57) are recorded and omitted from the join keys rather than pointed at a non-existent verse.
- **Attribution redistributed with the text:** "St. Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea: Commentary on the Four Gospels, Oxford translation ed. John Henry Newman (Rivington, 1841-45). Public domain."

### Roman Catechism (Catechism of the Council of Trent)
- **Text/Edition:** the *Catechism of the Council of Trent for Parish Priests* (the "Roman Catechism"), issued by order of Pope St. Pius V in 1566 as the Council of Trent's own catechism, in the English translation of the Rev. John A. McHugh, O.P. and Charles J. Callan, O.P., first published 1923 (Joseph F. Wagner, New York; B. Herder, London). This is the doctrinal spine of the corpus: the Church's post-Tridentine catechism, expounding the four pillars of catechesis (the Apostles' Creed, the Seven Sacraments, the Decalogue, and the Lord's Prayer).
- **License:** Public domain in the United States. The McHugh-Callan translation was published in 1923; all works published in the US before January 1, 1929 are in the public domain, and 1923 works specifically entered the public domain on January 1, 2019 (the 95-year term having run). The 1566 Latin original and its author (the Council of Trent, under Pius V) are centuries out of copyright. A faithful mechanical transcription of a public-domain text adds no new copyright. (This is a DIFFERENT work from the modern *Catechism of the Catholic Church*, which is copyright USCCB/LEV and is excluded below; the Roman Catechism of Trent is its own, older, public-domain text.)
- **Confirmed by:** The 1923 McHugh-Callan edition is hosted full-view (a status the Internet Archive reserves for public-domain works) at <https://archive.org/details/catechismofcounc0000pope>. An independent digital edition of the same translation, published by The Catholic Primer (catholicprimer.org), states on its own title page: "This Document is in the Public Domain," and dates the translation and preface to "John A. McHugh, O.P. and Charles J. Callan, O.P. (circa 1923)."
- **Source edition used for ingest:** catholicapologetics.info, which reproduces the McHugh-Callan text as clean per-section HTML: one page per structural unit (each Article, each Sacrament, each Commandment, each Petition, plus the four Part prologues), with an `<h3>` heading for every analytical subsection and `<p>` for each body paragraph. Retrieved 2026-07-10; the 42 source pages span `Preface.shtml`, `ApostlesCreed00-12`, `Holy7Sacraments*`, `TenCommandments*`, and `TheLordsPrayer*`. Every fetched page is cached under `ingest/.cache/catechism/` (gitignored), and the section URL is recorded in each node's `source.url`. The pages are Windows-1252 encoded and, apart from 311 soft-hyphen (U+00AD) markers and two stray OCR flecks (U+00B0, U+00B7), are pure ASCII. The soft-hyphens are the source's discretionary line-break hyphens, which render invisibly in a browser; removing them reproduces the source edition exactly as a reader sees it (so, for the handful the digitisation placed at a compound hyphen or a lost space, the corpus carries the same rendered form the source shows, e.g. "farfetched" where the 1923 print has "far-fetched" - a source-side digitisation artifact, not one introduced here).
- **Confirmed by (verbatim cross-check):** the ingested text was spot-checked sentence-for-sentence against a second, INDEPENDENT digitisation of the same McHugh-Callan translation (the saintsbooks.net / Catholic Primer PDF): sampled sentences from the Creed, the Sacraments, the Decalogue, and the Lord's Prayer are word-for-word identical, and the paragraph and subsection structure matches (e.g. Article I's 31 analytical subsections agree between the two). The text is a clean digitisation, not the mangled OCR of the old 1829 Donovan djvu scans (which were rejected as too corrupted to be verbatim). It does carry the occasional residual OCR slip inherited from its digitisation ancestry (for example "lo be of opinion" for "to be of opinion", a handful of "he" for "be", the odd apostrophe rendered as a comma) at a low rate of order one per several thousand words; these are cosmetic single-character slips, never abridgement, summary, or rewording, and are documented here honestly rather than silently. No paragraph is truncated or dropped, and the segments of every node rejoin to its `text` exactly, asserted at ingest and re-checked by `ingest/validate.py`.
- **Scope of what is ingested (an honest boundary, not a silent one):** the citable unit is the **subsection** - one headed analytical teaching unit within a Part (e.g. "Meaning Of This Article", "What They Forbid", "Why This Petition Is Placed First") - one node per subsection, 929 in all, across all four Parts and every one of the 12 Articles, 7 Sacraments, 10 Commandments, and 7 Petitions (completeness is asserted at ingest and fails loudly on any gap). Captured verbatim: every body paragraph, plus the structural unit-title heading (e.g. the full Creed clause or Commandment text that opens a unit page) folded losslessly into its first subsection rather than dropped, and only whitespace runs collapsed and soft-hyphens removed. Not captured as nodes: the site's navigation and page furniture, and the book's overall Preface / prefatory matter (the four Parts are the doctrinal spine; the general Preface is front matter, out of scope for this corpus, and remains one fetch away in the source). A necessary honest note on Scripture citations: this edition quotes Scripture inline WITHOUT chapter:verse references (the McHugh-Callan references are footnotes that every clean digitisation of the text drops), so unlike the Summa and Catena, `citations_out` is empty for this corpus and `data/graph/catechism_scripture_edges.json` / `catechism_index.json` are correspondingly empty. The citation machinery is built and correct; it will populate the moment a referenced edition is ingested. We deliberately did NOT reconstruct a citation graph from the footnotes of the OCR djvu digitisation, because only ~28% of subsection headings align cleanly there and a wrong citation is worse than a missing one for this project.
- **Attribution redistributed with the text:** "Catechism of the Council of Trent (The Roman Catechism), trans. John A. McHugh, O.P. and Charles J. Callan, O.P. (1923). Public domain."

### Douay-Rheims Bible (Challoner revision)
- **Translation:** Douay-Rheims, Challoner revision. Old Testament first published by the English College at Douay (1609-1610), New Testament at Rheims (1582), the whole revised and diligently compared with the Latin Vulgate by Bishop Richard Challoner, 1749-1752.
- **License:** Public domain. The Challoner text is from the 18th century; its author and revisers are centuries deceased and the work is far out of copyright everywhere. A faithful mechanical transcription of a public-domain text adds no new copyright.
- **Source edition used for ingest:** Project Gutenberg eBook #1581, *The Bible, Douay-Rheims, Complete* (the edition Gutenberg itself flags as the improved and more complete one, over #8300). Release date 2004, most recently updated 2026; digitization originally by Catholic Software (1999), released to Project Gutenberg as public domain. Retrieved 2026-07-09 from <https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1581> (plain-text file <https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/1581/pg1581.txt>).
- **Confirmed by:** Project Gutenberg distributes it under the Project Gutenberg License with US public-domain status; the underlying Challoner translation is public domain by age.
- **Canon and naming:** the full Catholic canon of 73 books, including the deuterocanonical books, in the Douay-Rheims naming convention (e.g. "1 Kings" = 1 Samuel, "3 Kings" = 1 Kings, "1 Paralipomenon" = 1 Chronicles, "1 Esdras" = Ezra, "Canticle of Canticles" = Song of Songs, "Ecclesiasticus" = Sirach, "Osee" = Hosea, "Apocalypse" = Revelation). Each verse node stores both the canonical modern citation used across Catena (e.g. `1 Samuel 2:2`) and the Douay book name as printed in this edition (`douay_book`), so nothing about the source naming is hidden.
- **Numbering:** Psalms and several other books follow the Septuagint/Vulgate numbering (as the Douay-Rheims does). This is intentional and correct for this corpus: the Summa's own Scripture citations (via New Advent) use the same Vulgate numbering, so verse text and citations align. A modern Bible would mismatch on the Psalms.
- **Scope of what is ingested (an honest boundary, not a silent one):** only the **verse text** is captured as corpus nodes, verbatim, one node per verse. Challoner's editorial apparatus that appears in the same source file - the per-book "arguments", the per-chapter summary headings, and the interspersed explanatory footnotes - is **not** included in the verse nodes, because the citable unit of Scripture is the verse, not the editor's commentary. That apparatus is public domain too and remains one fetch away in the source; it is simply out of scope for a verse corpus. No verse text is truncated or altered; only whitespace runs are collapsed, exactly as for the Summa.
- **Attribution redistributed with the text:** "The Holy Bible, Douay-Rheims Version, Challoner revision (1749-52). Public domain. Text via Project Gutenberg eBook #1581."

### Clementine Vulgate (Sixto-Clementine, 1592)
- **Edition:** the Sixto-Clementine Vulgate of 1592, the standard Latin text of the Roman Catholic Church for the four centuries after Trent. This is the Latin behind the corpus: the Summa quotes the Vulgate, and the Douay-Rheims was translated from it, so a Scripture citation now resolves to both the English and the Latin Aquinas actually read.
- **Electronic edition used for ingest:** The Clementine Vulgate Project (ed. Michael Tweedale et al., London, 2005), *Biblia Sacra juxta Vulgatam Clementinam*, a carefully proofread electronic transcription released by its editors into the public domain.
- **License:** Public domain. The 1592 Clementine text is centuries out of copyright, and the electronic edition's makers explicitly released their transcription to the public domain (they *request*, but do not by any license require, that users acknowledge the source, report typos, and flag modifications - requests we honour with this entry). A faithful mechanical transcription of a public-domain text adds no new copyright.
- **Source edition used for ingest:** the project's per-book `.lat` files, retrieved 2026-07-09 from the GitHub mirror <https://github.com/jrichter/ClementineVulgateConverter> (the `latin/` directory), whose README documents the files as downloaded from the Clementine Vulgate Project's own site (`vulsearch.sourceforge.net`, the project's original home before it moved to the Clementine Text Project). The mirror's own repository code is MIT-licensed; only the public-domain `.lat` text is used here, not that code.
- **Canon and naming:** the full Catholic canon of 73 books, in the edition's Latin abbreviations (`Gn`, `3Rg` = 1 Kings, `Ct` = Canticum Canticorum, `Sap` = Sapientia, `Sir` = Ecclesiasticus, `Apc` = Apocalypsis, ...). Each verse node stores the canonical modern citation shared across Catena (`John 1:14`) and the Latin book name as printed in the edition (`latin_book`, e.g. `Joannes`), and keys on the SAME `verse_key` as its Douay-Rheims counterpart, so one citation resolves to both texts.
- **Numbering:** Vulgate numbering throughout, the same scheme the Douay-Rheims and the Summa's own citations use. The two Bibles align verse-for-verse across 99.9% of addresses; the ~0.1% that do not meet are concentrated in the Psalms, where this Clementine edition and the Gutenberg Douay-Rheims split a handful of verses (chiefly the psalm tituli) differently. Both are valid Vulgate numbering; the gap is measured and recorded in the manifest, not forced.
- **Scope of what is ingested (an honest boundary, not a silent one):** only the **verse text** is captured, verbatim, one node per verse. The edition's own typographic markup that shares the source line is removed by an explicit, documented rule and nothing else is touched: poetic line (hemistich) breaks `/`, paragraph marks `\`, poetic-block brackets `[ ]`, and the Canticle's marginal speaker rubrics (`<Sponsa>`, `<Sponsus>`, `<Chorus>`), which are editorial apparatus identifying who speaks, not the inspired text. The Latin ligatures and diacritics of the edition (`æ`, `Æ`, `œ`, `ë`) are kept verbatim as UTF-8. No Latin word is altered; whitespace runs are collapsed, exactly as for the other texts. The validator re-derives every stored verse from the raw source under this same rule, so the transform is the only thing between source and corpus.
- **Attribution redistributed with the text:** "Biblia Sacra juxta Vulgatam Clementinam, electronic edition by The Clementine Vulgate Project (ed. M. Tweedale, 2005). Public domain."

## Excluded: copyrighted - permission track

### Catechism of the Catholic Church
- **Rights holder:** United States Conference of Catholic Bishops (USCCB) and Libreria Editrice Vaticana (LEV). Copyright on both the 1994 English and 1992 French translations.
- **The rule (USCCB):** use of fewer than 5,000 words does not require permission (with the copyright notice); **use of more than 5,000 words made available to the public at no cost requires written permission from the USCCB.** The full Catechism is ~180,000 words.
- **Source:** <https://www.usccb.org/committees/catechism/use-catechism-catholic-church>
- **Decision:** **We do NOT redistribute the Catechism text.** It is not in this corpus. We are pursuing written permission from USCCB for non-commercial, open, educational redistribution as a separate track (see `docs/` permission-request draft). Until and unless that permission is granted in writing, the Catechism stays out.
- **What we may still do without permission:** reference paragraph numbers and structure (facts, not the copyrighted expression), and quote individual paragraphs under the fair-use / <5,000-word allowance with the required copyright notice. Any such use carries the notice.

### Modern encyclical translations, modern Bible translations (RSV-CE, NABRE, ESV-CE, etc.)
- **License:** copyrighted. **Excluded.** Not redistributed. Use the public-domain Douay-Rheims / Vulgate instead, or pursue per-publisher permission.

## Provenance recorded per unit

Every ingested unit records, in its manifest entry: `source_url`, `source_edition`, `translator`, `license`, and `retrieved` (ISO date). This makes the whole corpus auditable back to where each word came from.
